Here are some CPE best practices which are known to improve CPE results in general:

- **Put yourself in the customer's shoes**! Think as if you were the customer getting help from a support organization addressing a business problem of yours all the way through.

- **Proper scoping, proper scoping, proper scoping**! With a clear scope, you'll have a good plan of how to move forward with troubleshooting the case and when to close the case without any conflicts with the customer. It also helps to set and meet the expectations of the customer in advance.

- The best scoping could be done with **good scoping questions** without leaving anything out from the very beginning.

- Plan your call with the customer, **think about it and write down the possible questions (scoping related or general) you may ask or be asked** during your call ahead of time.

- Make sure to find out **all relevant details with the scoping questions** including the customer's environment, history of the problem (any recent changes that lead to the problem), the reproduction steps, the frequency of the problem, any workarounds and why the workarounds are not useable, the impact of the problem in terms of number of affected users, clients or servers, down time and similar.

- **Listen, listen, and listen to the customer**! Customer treatment is important, and they would like to be heard and acknowledged.

- **Try to see the big picture at the very early stages of the case**, what exactly does the customer need? Why does the customer need that? What kind of business problem does the customer try to resolve with the current request in the case? Will what the customer tries to achieve serve their purpose and help address the business problem that they would like to resolve? Try to understand what makes the customer happy and fulfill our task?

- **Try to predict the possible reasons behind a certain customer reaction** in an e-mail or phone call by asking the right probing questions and act accordingly. Try to understand if there's a project deadline in place, if customer's own credibility is at risk, if the customer is also under pressure from upper management, if there's a cross team conflicts at the customer side, if the issue in hand will nullify a certain type of investment that the customer has already made, if the given customer contact has had bad support experience before, etc.

- **Avoid focusing on multiple problems in the same case** — it will negatively impact the CPE because it will take longer than usual and will lead to misperceptions on the customer side. Suggest the customer or the CSAM/IM to create a new case on behalf of the customer.

- **Provide easy to follow action plans for the customers**. Do not assume anything about customer's knowledge or skills when creating your action plans; they should be easy enough to implement without any questions back from the customer.

- **Do not share an action plan with the customer that you haven't tested yourself before**. Any unexpected results that may stem from the execution of the action plan may lead to operational problems in the customer environment and may negatively affect your credibility.

- Whenever sharing the findings with the customers, **provide as many details as possible (screenshots, outputs from logs collected from the customer environment)** which will show your technical expertise so that they will know that we utilize every log we ask for to find out the root cause.

- **Justify the actions that you suggest** (like changing a registry setting, installing or uninstalling a component and similar) so that the customers won't question why they have to take that given action.

- **If the customer is ignoring you or your action plans**, questioning everything you recommend, please immediately engage your TA or the escalation team. Involving the CSAM is also important in such situations.

- Keep the customer **up to date** on the progress in the case and always respond on time. Do not let the customer chase you with an update request e-mail or a callback request.

- **Always keep the customers informed about your availability** and do a proper handover when needed (vacations, training, and similar).

- **Collaboration, collaboration, collaboration**! Good collaboration is one of the most important keys to having high CPE results. Some critical points: NOT waiting too long to ask for help and tracking the internal collaboration progress all the time.

- If you depend on others' feedback or assistance, **you should still own the case** — you're the driver and it's your responsibility to make sure that the case progresses. We're **ONE Microsoft** and we cannot simply blame any internal parties for anything in customer communications.

- **Join triages** to discuss your cases, follow relevant discussion aliases (DLs) to extend your skills, and most importantly your network of experts.

- **Consider contacting relevant discussion aliases** when you want to get feedback from a broader internal audience. These DLs often have members from Product groups.

- **Always be the driver of the case** instead of letting the customer drive it.

- **Be polite and calm** all the time and do not enter any sort of arguments with the customers. Think about engaging the TA, CSAM and your manager to address such situations.

- **Keep your voice tone under control all the time**. At the end of the day, you're a support professional representing Microsoft.

- Avoid any kind of **sensitive discussions** with the customers: Politics, Race, Gender, Nationality, Religion and similar.

- **Body language**: For live meetings with video calls or face to face communications — Maintain eye contact, Practice good posture, Smile when appropriate, Avoid unnecessary movement, Keep an open stance, Speak loudly/clearly/confidently, Be an active listener.

- **Avoid using informal communication language** on the phone or in e-mails; make sure to use your signatures in all customer communications.

- **Make a final call to the customer before archiving the case** and verify the customer is satisfied with the work done and agrees with the case closure with no pending actions.

- **Always send a case closure e-mail showing your technical expertise** and the work you've done on that specific case. Provide as much detail as possible detailing the technical progress made in the case.

- Encourage the customer to fill out **the survey** before archiving the case.

- **When closing cases temporarily**, always mention that the case could be re-opened any time later on and it's just a matter of replying to the e-mail thread.

- **Be extra cautious when dealing with re-opened cases**. Re-sync with the customer and provide a quick summary of the latest situation.

- **Do not pronounce the word "bug"** in customer communications. Use terms like "software defect", "unexpected software behavior" and similar.

- **For 3rd party issues, don't leave the customer alone** and don't try to close the case right away. Try to make the customer feel that "we're with you here as long as you think our presence might be useful."

- **Be the customer's lawyer** in interactions with 3rd party vendor support.

- **Critsits** require special attention. Sometimes creating an internal or customer Teams channel might be useful to quickly exchange status updates.

- **Plan your day ahead of time** to prioritize your work for the given day. Priority should be your calls and scheduled meetings. Then plan on what cases to prioritize troubleshooting (analyzing logs, researching, action plan creation and similar).

- **Plan your week ahead of time** to review your weekly schedules (customer calls, trainings, other internal meetings).

- Avoid **excuses** as much as possible.

- Get prepared for calls where you need to deliver **some bad news** to the customer.

- **Know yourself and raise a flag** whenever you feel overloaded, overwhelmed or under pressure.

- **Know your limits in terms of your work** like how many calls you can handle per day, how many logs from different cases you can analyze per day and similar.
